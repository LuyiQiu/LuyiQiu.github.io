About

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Applied Physics at Harvard University, working with Ariel Amir. My research is in theoretical biophysics and soft condensed matter, with interests in the physics of living systems, cellular mechanics, and nonequilibrium phenomena.

My work combines physical modeling, elasticity theory, stochastic processes, and applied mathematics to study biological systems across scales. Recent projects include chromosome segregation, spindle checkpoint dynamics, and the mechanics and morphology of bacterial cells.

Previously, I received my B.Sc. in Physics from Peking University.

Research

Broadly, I am interested in how physical principles shape biological function. My current and recent research themes include:

  • Chromosome segregation and first-passage problems in cell division.
  • Spindle assembly checkpoint dynamics and error-correction mechanisms.
  • Mechanical instabilities in rod-shaped bacteria.
  • Pressure-induced morphology and wrinkling in biological and elastic tubular structures.
  • Shape formation in helical bacterial cells.
